## **What Are Co-Occurring Disorders?**

Co-occurring disorders, sometimes called dual diagnosis, happen when a person
experiences both a substance use disorder and a mental health condition. These
conditions may appear together in many different ways.

For example, someone may struggle with alcohol misuse and depression. Another
person may experience anxiety and prescription drug dependence. Someone else
may use substances to cope with trauma, panic attacks, bipolar symptoms, or
overwhelming stress.

### **Why the Two Conditions Often Overlap**

Addiction and mental health conditions often interact like waves pushing
against each other. Substance use may temporarily dull emotional pain, but
over time, it can intensify symptoms. Mental health struggles may make it
harder to stop using substances, especially when drugs or alcohol feel like
the only available relief.

That is why professional treatment should look deeper than the surface. At
Florida Addiction and Recovery Center, we work to understand the root causes,
not just the visible behaviors.

## **Why Co-Occurring Disorders Can Be Hard to Identify**

Many people do not immediately realize they are dealing with co-occurring
disorders. They may think they only have an addiction problem. Others may
believe their substance use is simply a reaction to stress, anxiety,
depression, or trauma.

The truth is often more layered. A person may use alcohol to calm racing
thoughts. They may misuse prescription medication to sleep. They may turn to
drugs to escape painful memories. Over time, what began as coping can become
dependency.

### **The Cycle Can Feel Confusing**

Imagine trying to put out a fire while another spark keeps landing nearby.
That is what untreated co-occurring disorders can feel like. Substance use may
cause emotional instability, and the emotional instability may increase the
urge to use.

Without integrated treatment, people may feel stuck in a frustrating loop.

## **Common Mental Health Conditions Linked to Addiction**

Co-occurring disorders can involve many types of mental health concerns. No
two people are the same, so treatment should always be personalized.

### **Anxiety Disorders**

Anxiety can feel like an alarm that will not turn off. Some people use
alcohol, benzodiazepines, marijuana, or other substances to calm their nerves.
While substances may offer temporary relief, they can make anxiety worse over
time.

### **Depression**

Depression can drain motivation, energy, and hope. A person may use substances
to feel numb, distracted, or temporarily uplifted. Unfortunately, substance
use can deepen sadness, isolation, and fatigue.

### **Trauma and PTSD**

Trauma can leave emotional bruises that do not fade easily. People may use
substances to avoid flashbacks, nightmares, shame, fear, or hypervigilance.
Treating trauma with care and patience is often an important part of long-term
recovery.

### **Bipolar Disorder**

Bipolar disorder may involve intense mood shifts, impulsivity, depression, or
periods of elevated energy. Substance use can complicate symptoms and make
stability more difficult.

### **Panic Disorders and Stress-Related Conditions**

Panic attacks can feel frightening and physically intense. Some people begin
relying on substances to prevent or escape panic symptoms. With treatment,
healthier coping strategies can be developed.

## **Substance Use Concerns That May Appear With Mental Health Disorders**

Co-occurring disorders may involve alcohol, opioids, prescription medications,

At Florida Addiction and Recovery Center, we understand that people rarely

### **Alcohol Use and Emotional Pain**

Alcohol is commonly used to relax, sleep, socialize, or forget. But when
drinking becomes a way to manage depression, anxiety, or trauma, the
relationship with alcohol can become unhealthy.

### **Prescription Medication Misuse**

Some individuals develop dependence on medications prescribed for pain,
anxiety, or sleep. The situation can be especially confusing because the

### **Drug Use and Escapism**

Substances can become a shortcut away from pain, but shortcuts often come with
hidden costs. Over time, escape can turn into dependence, and dependence can
make mental health symptoms harder to manage.
